How I Used Agents to Plan It

A small build log on reading a repo, extracting a workflow, and turning that workflow into a product idea.

I wanted to do something a little more honest than writing a repo summary.

It is easy to read a repository, say "this is interesting," and stop there. I wanted to see whether the structure inside the repo could be lifted out, turned into a product pipeline, and then tested on a concrete idea.

So the process became three moves:

  1. read the repo seriously,
  2. extract a reusable pipeline,
  3. use that pipeline to shape a small product.

Step 1: read for structure, not trivia

When I looked through Matt Pocock’s skills repo, I tried not to get distracted by individual skill names. The more useful question was: what kind of work is this repo trying to make easier, and in what order?

That changed the reading a lot.

Instead of seeing a set of prompts, I started seeing a chain: clarify, write, plan, break down, implement, triage, publish.

That was the first useful compression.

Step 2: turn the chain into a pipeline

Once that pattern was visible, the next move was to re-express it in cleaner product language. The result was a lightweight pipeline:

  • discovery,
  • spec,
  • UX,
  • architecture,
  • task breakdown,
  • implementation,
  • evaluation,
  • publication.

I like this version because it is simple enough to remember and specific enough to use. Each stage asks for a small artifact. Each one narrows the next.

Step 3: pressure-test it with a real product idea

A pipeline is only interesting if it changes what gets built. So the next step was to apply it to a real example.

I chose a bookkeeping app because the problem is familiar and annoying. Small operators have to clean transactions, categorize them, chase exceptions, and prepare things for tax season. It is repetitive, boring, and still full of judgment calls.

That made it a good place to ask: where should the human sit, and where should the system carry more weight?

What the process clarified

The best thing about this workflow was not speed. It was separation.

One pass looked at the repo itself. Another pass translated the repo into a reusable pipeline. Another pass asked what kind of bookkeeping product would actually fit that pipeline.

That separation prevented a common failure mode: mixing framework excitement, product speculation, and implementation details all into one blurry draft.

What came out the other side

By the end, three things were clearer than they were at the start:

  • the repo is best understood as a workflow system, not a prompt collection,
  • a lightweight product pipeline can be expressed in a stable sequence of artifacts,
  • the bookkeeping app should be built around exception review, not around making the user do every clerical step manually.

That is a much stronger result than simply saying the repo was inspiring.

What I like about working this way

I think this is where agent-based planning starts to feel useful. Not when one system tries to do the whole job in one dramatic leap. But when the work is split into a few clean lines of thought, and then brought back together by someone who still has to choose.

That keeps the process exploratory without becoming messy. It also makes the final writing better, because each article is anchored in an actual decision trail.

Why I wanted to publish the process too

I did not want to publish only the conclusion. The process matters here because the process is part of the claim.

If the argument is that good skills and good pipelines are really about clean handoffs, then it makes sense to show the handoffs.

So these three posts belong together:

  • one about the repo and what it teaches,
  • one about the product idea it helped shape,
  • and one about the path between them.

That feels truer to the work.
