What AI Still Cannot Replace in Game Design

A short summary of a thoughtful article about AI, logic, emotion, and the real work of game design.

I read an interesting article asking a simple question: if AI keeps getting better, what should a game designer still train in order not to fall behind?

The article’s answer is clear. The durable skill is not only writing plans or generating ideas. It is the ability to take a player’s emotional experience and trace it back to concrete design causes.

The author begins from a familiar point: large language models are powerful, but they are still systems built on probability, pattern, and logic. They can produce reasonable output. They can imitate emotion. But entertainment is not only about logic. Games exist to create feelings.

That means the important question for a designer is not just how to describe a game, but how to understand why a player feels something in a specific moment.

The article gives a useful example. A player is searching a safe in a dangerous area and suddenly hears footsteps. Their palms sweat. Their heartbeat rises. They feel tension. A weak analysis stops there and says, “this part is exciting.” A stronger analysis keeps going. Why is it exciting? Because reward is at stake. Because the player is exposed. Because information is incomplete. Because sound arrives at the exact moment the player’s attention is narrowed.

This is the real point of the article. Good game design is not only about having ideas. It is about explaining experience. A designer needs to break a feeling into causes that can be built, tested, and adjusted.

From that view, AI may become more and more useful for documentation, structuring options, comparing systems, and speeding up production. But the harder human work remains: sensing emotion accurately, then translating it into design.

The article ends in a practical place. Communication still matters. Game development is collaborative work. So the future designer still needs both emotional diagnosis and the ability to explain it clearly to other people.

The summary, in one line, is simple: AI can help produce game design work, but one of the deepest skills still belongs to the person who can explain why a player feels what they feel.

AI 还替代不了游戏策划里的哪一层

一篇关于 AI、理性逻辑、情绪感受和游戏策划核心能力的简短摘要。

我刚读到一篇很有意思的文章，它问了一个很直接的问题：如果 AI 继续变强，游戏策划到了 2026 年最该培养什么能力，才不会被落下？

文章给出的答案很明确：真正不容易被替代的，不只是会写方案、会产出点子，而是把玩家的情绪体验，追溯到具体设计原因的能力。

文章先从一个常见判断开始：大语言模型确实很强，但它本质上还是建立在概率、模式和理性逻辑之上的系统。它可以给出合理输出，可以模仿情绪表达，但娱乐产品最终服务的不是逻辑本身，而是人的感受。游戏也是一样。

所以对策划来说，真正重要的问题不只是“怎么描述一个游戏”，而是“为什么玩家会在这一刻产生这种感受”。

文里举了一个很好的例子。玩家在危险区域里搜保险箱，突然听见脚步声，开始手心出汗、心跳加快、感到紧张。比较弱的分析会停在“这里很刺激”。更强的分析会继续问：为什么刺激？因为有收益在风险中，因为玩家暴露了自己，因为信息不完整，因为声音在一个玩家注意力刚好被收窄的时刻出现。

这正是文章最关键的地方。好的游戏设计不只是有创意，而是能够解释体验。设计者需要把一种感受拆成可以被构建、测试、调整的具体成因。

从这个角度看，AI 当然会越来越擅长帮忙写文档、整理选项、比较系统、加快产出。但更难、也更像人的那部分工作还在：更准确地感受情绪，再把它翻译成设计。

文章最后也落回一个很实际的位置：沟通能力依然重要。游戏开发本来就是协作工作。所以未来的策划仍然需要两种能力：一是情绪诊断，二是把这种诊断清楚地讲给团队里其他人听。

如果把全文压成一句话，大概就是：AI 可以帮助生产游戏设计工作，但最深的一层，依然属于那个能够解释“玩家为什么会有这种感受”的人。
