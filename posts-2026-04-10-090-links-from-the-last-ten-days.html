Recent Links, in Plain Words

A short human summary of the links I sent over the last ten days, and what they were really about.

Over the last ten days, the links I sent were not random. They kept circling around the same few questions: how agents should be built, how knowledge should accumulate, how history gets misremembered, and what kind of work still feels alive.

Here is the short version, in plain words.

1. Anthropic — Managed Agents

https://www.anthropic.com/engineering/managed-agents

This piece is really saying: stop treating an agent like one magic box.

The useful split is:

  • the brain thinks,
  • the hands do things,
  • the session remembers what happened.

In human words: if you want agents to run for a long time, recover from failure, and work across many environments, you have to separate thinking, doing, and memory.

2. Karpathy — llm-wiki

https://gist.github.com/karpathy/442a6bf555914893e9891c11519de94f

This one is about knowledge.

The point is simple: do not make the model rediscover everything every time you ask a question. Let it slowly build and maintain a wiki.

In human words: instead of endlessly chatting over raw material, turn the good parts into pages that can stay, grow, and connect to each other.

3. Tom Holland video — Persia’s forgotten influence

https://youtu.be/CFUtwYXE5Vs?si=OgwtQDI9eqPZMXuM

This video is not just saying that Persia was important. It is saying that history is usually told too neatly.

We are often taught a clean story where “the West” grows by itself. But real history is messier. Civilizations shape each other through rivalry, borrowing, imitation, and contact.

In human words: even the people who say they are different from you may still be deeply shaped by you.

4. Matt Pocock’s skills repo

https://github.com/mattpocock/skills/tree/main

I kept coming back to this one because it does not feel like a bag of prompts. It feels like a workflow for building products.

The hidden shape is something like:

  • figure out the problem,
  • turn it into a spec,
  • break it into issues,
  • design, build, test, and refine,
  • then package what worked.

In human words: good skills are not just clever outputs. They give work a better shape.

What all of these links were really about

They look different on the surface. One is about agents. One is about wiki systems. One is about ancient history. One is about software workflow.

But underneath, they were all touching the same theme:

important systems are not one-shot performances.

  • They need structure.
  • They need memory.
  • They need layers.
  • They need accumulation.
  • They are shaped by contact, not born in isolation.

That applies to agents. It applies to knowledge systems. It even applies to civilizations.

My short takeaway

Over these ten days, I think I was really circling one question:

How do you build something that does not just answer once, but keeps becoming itself over time?

That might be an agent. That might be a wiki. That might be a workflow. That might even be a culture.

That is what made these links feel connected.
